ReSTIR

For the second part of the project, I explored and attempted to implement ReSTIR with spatial reuse in my path tracer. Unfortunately, I encountered a bug in the reservoir combine step that I was unable to debug due to time constraints. As a result, the implementation for this part uses Resampled Importance Sampling (RIS) with Weighted Reservoir Sampling instead of full ReSTIR.
Below are the direct light contribution results using RIS with different sample counts per pixel. From left to right, the sample counts are 1, 32, and 128 samples per pixel (spp). At 1 spp, the image exhibits extreme noise, with many light sources barely contributing to the final image. This occurs because of the low probability of sampling important light paths with such sparse sampling. As the sample count increases to 32 spp, the sampling process captures more significant light contributions, reducing noise and making shadows and specular highlights more coherent. At 128 spp, the image shows much smoother illumination and cleaner reflections, indicating convergence toward a low-variance estimate.
This behavior occurs because weighted reservoir sampling in RIS selects light samples based on their contribution weight, but requires sufficient candidate samples to make effective choices. At low sample counts, many important light directions are missed, particularly for glossy surfaces or small light sources. As the sample count increases, the estimator becomes more robust by capturing more representative samples and producing more stable lighting results. This demonstrates RIS's strength in balancing importance sampling with adaptive sample selection, showing that its effectiveness improves significantly with increased sample count.
